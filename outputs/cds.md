# FDA’s 2026 CDS Guidance: What changes for hospital pharmacy governance

<!-- SOURCE_METADATA_START -->
> **Source article:** Clinical Decision Support Software
> **Published:** 2026-01-29
<!-- SOURCE_METADATA_END -->

## Quick Take
- The FDA’s January 29, 2026 guidance, Clinical Decision Support Software, clarifies when software used by health care professionals (HCPs) is excluded from the medical device definition under section 520(o)(1)(E) of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act. It is nonbinding guidance but signals how the FDA will interpret the boundary.
- Many familiar inpatient tools can still qualify as non-device clinical decision support (CDS): order sets, drug–drug interaction alerts, duplicate-test notifications, formulary guidance, medication‑reconciliation alerts, antibiotic-selection support, and IV‑fluid recommendations based on daily labs.
- The core tension for pharmacy is whether vendors and internal teams can demonstrate sufficient transparency, explainability, and validation so that high-value tools remain non-device without introducing unsafe automation or procurement drag.

## Key Details
- To qualify as Non‑Device CDS, a software function must meet all four statutory criteria. Most important for pharmacy: it must not acquire, process, or analyze medical images, in vitro diagnostic signals, or patterns/signals from signal‑acquisition systems.
- FDA examples of device‑level inputs include CT, MRI, ultrasound, pathology images, electrocardiogram waveforms, next‑generation sequencing (NGS) data, and continuous glucose monitor readings.
- The guidance draws a practical line between signal‑level inputs and discrete clinical information. Single results — e.g., potassium 4.0 mmol/L, glucose 95 mg/dL, or blood pressure 120/80 mmHg — are generally treated as medical information when used consistently with labeling.
- Non‑Device CDS may display, analyze, or print patient information and reference sources (guidelines, textbooks, approved labeling, peer‑reviewed literature, government recommendations). It may support recommendations to HCPs so long as it does not replace or direct clinical judgment.
- Functions the FDA continues to treat as device‑regulated include image or waveform analysis, software that produces a definitive diagnosis, patient- or caregiver‑facing CDS, and time‑sensitive intervention tools (for example, sepsis or stroke alerts, heart‑attack detection, cesarean timing support, and insulin dose calculation).

## Why it matters
- Patient safety: the fourth statutory criterion—ability of clinicians to independently review the basis for a recommendation—is the practical governance test. Failure here raises automation‑bias risks at ordering, verification, and treatment selection.
- Informatics and workflow: expect the highest risk in tools that appear simple in workflow but depend on continuous signals, trend interpretation, or highly specific recommendations. Pharmacy leaders may need a complete CDS inventory mapped by input type and output specificity.
- Vendor and contract diligence: purchasing conversations may shift toward documentation of intended use, user population, required inputs and data quality, algorithm development and validation, and representativeness of training/validation data. That raises diligence needs and could slow contracting.
- Adoption and implementation: for high‑impact CDS, even if non‑device, local usability testing and pilot evidence may be necessary to demonstrate safety, clinician understanding, and measurable ROI.

## Bottom Line
Early signal — immediate governance implications, not a stop order. Review high‑impact pharmacy and electronic health record (EHR) decision support by input source, recommendation specificity, and explainability before the next vendor renewal, build, or pilot.
